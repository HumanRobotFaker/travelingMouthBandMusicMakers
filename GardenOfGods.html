Garden of Gods

The first-born female offspring from the Tree of Mammalia, which is supposedly an infinite breasted tree toward which newly liberated souls (i.e. “dead”) naturally gravitate. Initially the attraction is sexual and souls will interact likewise. But once the mouth latches onto the nipple, the tree lactates its milk of amnesia, which overwhelms the sensual faculties of the soul. Then the tree absorbs the soul, leading to a flower-to-fruiting progression that causes the soul to fall like a seed back to terrestrial existence. Where the seed lands is where the soul reincarnates.

Statue is named Morgan Conduit and plaque depicts that it is believed that all sentient creatures derive from the asexual reproduction of this deity. Individual differences are the result of “wayward-traveling particle

obstruction” causing “communication interruptions” that lead to construction errors. This led to the formation of different biological sexes, the chaos of meiotic cell division, and all the individuality that currently exists.

Recurring dream figure in Dr. Don Dalton’s marathon lucid dream (he goes by the name Azüul). He is always shouting, panicking, and trying to distract Dr. Don Dalton from the fact that he’s asleep in a dream realm of his own creation over which he has total control.

Plaque mentions that Azüul can manifest in many different forms. At the beginning of a lucid dreamer’s journey to increased lucidity, Azüul will not possess a fixed form but will manifest in a myriad of different shapes. Nonetheless, when steady lucidity is achieved, Azüul will solidify into a particular identification and persona that is unique to every individual dreamer. The rhyme and reason behind each particular molding is unknown; although, naturally, many individuals have their own personal theories.

Statue crudely depicts a sprite-like figure named Tû, who runs underneath the legs of individuals attempting to move, causing them to trip, stumble, and/or fall. Tû does not perform such acts out of maliciousness, but rather, Tû is lost in their own particular game of hide-and-seek, and is either attempting to hide and flee from themself or – contrarily – attempting to pursue and find themself.

In the garden, the small statue is placed underneath prosthetic legs that have been covered with an aesthetic duct-tape designed to resemble the patterns within a wood grain. No explanation is given behind why the legs are to appear in such a fashion, but it is believed to be connected to the aforementioned mythology connecting mammalia to autotrophs.  

Statue depicts the immolation and death by boiling water of the famous prophet Yeshi in a giant cauldron populated with common criminals.  

Plaque mentions typical yeshian belief that Yeshi was a divine incarnation of Ahuramazda, whose divine essence remained in his corporeal form even upon perishing, whereby it began to leach into the environment post-burial and decomposition. Henceforth, the divine essence has miraculously expanded in “volume, mass, and influential magnitude” in a logarithmic fashion.

The tetra-twin effect features two separate statues consisting of an identical (albeit geometrically reflected) composition of four figures standing upon a common substrate.

A large plaque in small letters explains the following lore: when four individuals of varied (but particular) stature assemble together, a tangent universe is created where these twinned tetra-ensemble prance off to behave either identically or differently than the original from which they offsprung. The significance of this supposed phenomenon is not explained nor elaborated upon.

This portal deity, known as Ingurgis, inhales ethereal spooks that possess animal minds and bodies. This powerful intake of spirits that haunt souls is not for refashioning purposes or reasons akin to metabolic conversion, but rather, it is a simple cause-and-effect from a force akin to a gravitational black hole.

The plaque draws attention to the fact that the entrancing portal is located in the cavern of Ingurgis’ chest. There are multiple mythologies surrounding the creation of this ultra-attractive cavern within Ingurgis’ chest. Some tales tell of great sorrow from the death of a loved one (wife or child). Some tales tell of a hedonistic desire to gobble up all wondrous essences known to mankind, until the mass of all that was intook caused a collapse in the fabric of existence, thus ushering in a phenomenon akin to a black hole.

Optis entrances humans with beauty. It is a simple concept: that which is so beautiful, one’s gaze is automatically toward it. Optis distracts onlookers from the majesty of the rest of the world, but most viewers don’t mind or even know what they’ve missed.

It is unknown whether this tribe gathered the statue through barter, conquest, or whether it is in fact an artifact of a different culture who coexisted and/or conquered the tribe.
