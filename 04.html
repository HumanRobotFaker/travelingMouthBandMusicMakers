IV.

     “Let’s clean this house,” Mars said.

     “Wait, what?”

     “You ever leave on vacation and return to a dirty house?”

     “No.”

     “I have. It’s so much less nice than coming home to a clean house,” and she started picking up random dirty dishes lying about the house.

     “But isn’t this the work for Hector’s family?” I asked.

     “You’re his friend, right?” Mars said. “You know his story, right?” And I did. Dad died when Hector was a toddler (single-car accident on wintry rural road, driving home from a [bar]) and Mom was looney-tunes crazy, an emotional tornado of fury whenever she was around, which was rarely. Thus, Hector and his brothers lived in squalor and filth, in a house that used to be perfectly nice, until one-by-one a zillion small things broke and never got fixed.

     So I helped her clean. I mean, I crushed on her, so there wasn’t any real choice, but, just to be clear, she never showed any sign of romantic interest in me.

     “Did you and Hector date?” I asked.

     “Before he dated Britni, we saw a play and he felt me up. I mean, right before he and Britni dated. Like two days later, I looked at his [site on] InfiniSpace and they were in a relationship.” She turned on the sink water and began attacking the pyramid of dirty, mismatched dishes. “I thought about telling Britni, but my mom advised me not to.”

     “You told your mom?”

     “I tell my mom everything. My dad, not so much.”

     “How come?”

     “That’s how us Maniis do. Mothers raise and educate the children; Fathers stay out of the way.” Mars handed me a wet, clean dish and I searched every drawer to find a clean cloth to dry it with. “Anyhow,” she continued, “that’s before I decided to be done with dating men. They’re biologically predisposed to be faithless.”

     My heart dropped in my chest. “That’s very confusing.”

     “Huh?”

     “I recently read your essay on gender nihilism.”

     She looked at me. Then she looked back down at the dish she was scrubbing. “Well, gender is different than biological sex.”

     “I mean, I get that, but I don’t get how one can be a gender nihilist yet dismiss a whole gender – or biological sex – from romantic consideration.”

     “Well, I never said I was a gender nihilist. I merely wrote a reflection upon the concept. And then there was silence for a while, as Mars grabbed a hard plastic widget (a broken piece of spatula) and used it to break up the hardened food adhered to the bottom of a pan. “Men’s biology makes them not take sex seriously. It’s simple math and logick: they have one gazillion [gametes] and zero percent chance that the action leads to a biological mass growing inside them.”

     “So we’re slaves of biology? Isn’t that the argument for chauvinism?”

     “It can be,” she said. “But I think you’re misrepresenting me. You’re taking my transient thoughts and cementing them so you can make a statue of them.”

     I laughed. I dropped it. We kept cleaning. My iContactsTM lit up with autoAlgoAdvisors encouraging me to keep cleaning.
