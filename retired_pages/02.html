II.

     The procedures [(for attempting Dr. Dalton’s perpetual dive into the dream realm)] were easily accessible on the interwebs, if one took the effort to procure the equipment and chemicals for the task at hand. Luckily, we were biking distance from Senor de la Luna’s Lunar Emporium, an outpost for scientific activity that was largely subsidized by Traveling Mouth Band University , of which we were all “students”. Señor de la Luna was there, per usual, [fooling] around with a circular configuration of magnets that he’d liberated from the motors and speakers of trashed electronics.

     Winfield hovered his head over a stretch of magnets that’d be epoxied to the floor with their north poles upward. “MagLevTrain?” he asked.

     “MagLevTrain,” Señor de la Luna responded.

     “The kids’ll love that,” Hector said. “Electromagnetism is like magick.”

     “Life is magick,” Señor de la Luna said. “Why do you request [the sleeping chemical compound]?”

     Hector handed him a paper-copy of the supposed scientific inquiry on hand. It declared an interest in somatic cell shedding of incessantly sleeping organisms in contrast to their normally sleeping counterparts. Señor de la Luna turned the page and suddenly sang, “Woooaaahhh…”

     “What?”

     “That’s a lot of reptiles. Are you sure you don’t want to start with something more modest?”

     Hector shrugged. “Sample size is everything.”

     Uriah interrupted. “How does [the sleeping chemical compound] work? I mean, biochemically?”

     Señor de la Luna took out a pen and began writing on a piece of paper. “You know how red blood cells carry a hemoglobin protein that is the perfect shape to hold an oxygen molecule?”

     “Umm… yeah?” Uriah said.

     “For yeshi's meat,” Winfield said, punching Uriah in the shoulder.

     “Ow!” Uriah cried.

     “Well,” Señor de la Luna continued, “if a similarly shaped molecule – like carbon monoxide, for example – gets in there first, then the oxygen molecule is blocked out. It’s like that, except it’s in the brain and this molecule blocks up the port that would accept the natural molecule that would spur an animal awake.” He set down his pen. “Speaking of which: the mass of these laboratory reptiles just happens to add up to 75 kilograms, which could be the mass of any of you given teenagers.”

     “Señor de la Luna,” Winfield said, “it’s against the TMB bylaws to utilize drugs in human experimentation.”

     “Exactly,” he said. “And I have liability to worry about. I’m only authorizing and distributing for a quarter of this sample size.”

     “But Señor de la Luna!” Hector cried. “That sort of sample size won’t be taken seriously by peer review.”

     And then Señor de la Luna laughed. And he laughed. And then Yeshi appeared, smiling on the top of the industrial freezer against the northern wall.
