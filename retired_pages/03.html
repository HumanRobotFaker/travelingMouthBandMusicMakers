III.

     Hector never intended to receive his whole supply from the LunarEmporium; diversification was the name of the game in all formats as far as TMB seemed to be concerned. Most of Hector’s sleeping chemicals derived from a friend of a friend’s adopted sister who was finishing up pharmacologySkool and in the past “suffered from insomnia”.

     The glass casket was quickly assembled by Andrew Ramblehurst and his league of AntarcticSkouts (which included Uriah Bleu). Then they returned to their work of assembling a hiking trail to Jakob Rum’s garden of the gods, which rested at an ignored plot of land near where BeaverCreekTownship and XeniaTownship overlapped.

     Hector laid in the glass casket, which had been lightly cushioned and pillowed, and tried to get comfortable. MarsEid, who had juvenile diabetes and thus was comfortable with needles, attached a [sterile] port to one of Hector’s accessible veins, and then made sure the IV drip bag operated properly. MarsEid didn’t “attend” TMB, but she could’ve if she wasn’t so convinced that it was a neo-fascist skull-measuring enterprise from the depths of Kur.

     “Triple check these figures,” she said to me, passing over a sheet of calculations. I looked at them and then independently verified them with pen-and-paper, because we’d left our iContactsTM “charging” in the other room because we assumed this whole activity to be highly illegal (operating medicine without a medical license).

     “Looks right to me,” I said. “I got the same numbers.”

     She looked down at Hector, who was smiling up at her.

     “You’re an idiot,” she said.

     “Thank you so much,” he said.

     She attached the EEG stickers and then the EKG stickers to Hector, while I verified the monitors. Then she touched his cheek softly. “Sweet dreams.”

     He closed his eyes and went to sleep. I quietly closed the lid of the casket, perforated with breathing holes.
