XIX.

     “What number did you get?” Tamaya asked me at the Lunar Emporium.

     “Three,” I said.

     “Impressive,” she said. “Shadow number 720.”

     “What are the odds?” I said. Then I thought about the stack of playing cards. “I believe the odds were 1 out of 40.”

     “1 out of 38,” she said. “They don’t let people get a number one or a number 2160.”

     I sat on the couch, my eyeball pupils twitching every which way, putting together this DeepFake of Prince-o’-pull Cox that I projected to the LCDTV hanging on the wall. The dataset was too small, so the DeepFake wasn’t convincing, but Andrew said it was better that way. “Realism is overrated,” he said.

     Somewhere behind us, Señor de la Luna and Jakob Rums hot-glued magnets down to plywood. One after another, in a straight line, for the magick train that would levitate off the ground.

     “My number originally was fifteen, but if you go through the solidification ceremony, you get to pick your own number,” Tamaya said. “Now I’m sixty.”

     “I’m 432,” Jakob said. “I just thought it was the coolest number of the set.”

     “Señor de la Luna,” Tamaya said, turning her head backward. “What number are you?”

     “Oh, I don’t know,” de la Luna responded. “It was a long time ago.”

     Tamaya turned back toward the LCDTV, where Prince-o’-pull Cox pointed menacingly at the pseudo-camera. “I bet you were a 4 or a 540. That’s why we’re all here today.”

     “Why do you think that?” I asked. “What do those numbers mean?”

     “The numbers don’t mean anything in isolation,” she said. “They only mean anything together.”

     Jakob laughed loudly from across the room. “Don’t mean to be mean, but it don’t mean anything!”

     “So why 4 or 540?” I asked again.

     “Because if you take everyone’s smaller number, multiply them all together, and you get 2160, then you’ve got a special group.” Then she started pointing: “You’re 3, Jakob’s shadow is 5, my shadow is 36, and if either of Señor de la Luna’s numbers are 4, then that makes 2160. What number did Andrew draw?”

     “He refused,” I said. “He got all weird at times. He wouldn’t dip his hands in the heated hemisphere of water either.”

     “Probably a pseudo-atheist,” Tamaya said.

     “Pseudo?”

     “Yeah, you know, if they truly didn’t believe, then they wouldn’t have any problem just doing the rituals.”

     Jakob laughed. “I don’t think that’s it.”

     “What do you think it is then?” Tamaya asked.

     “I’m sorry, ma’am, but that’s top secret information integral to Antarctica’s National Security.”

     On the LCDTV screen, Principal Cox said: “Spare the rod; spoil the child!”

     Tamaya turned to me. “Are you writing his speech?”

     “Not really,” I said. “I’m deferring to an AI, which spits out something called ‘predictions’ and I either validate or deny them.” And I [selected] validate.

     “Children are to be seen, not heard,” LCDTV Cox said. I [selected] deny.

     “So you operant condition it,” Tamaya said. “Like a rat.”

     “Basically.”

     Tamaya returned to her handmade propaganda art pieces, furiously coloring on small lineless index cards, covering every white space, before running it through the emporium’s plastic laminator machine. Then she handed me a card. It read: “I am not ruled by rules made by rulers.”

     “Pretty good,” I said.

     LCDTV Cox said, “I hereby reintroduce the act of spanking as a means of correction for the disruly child.” I [selected] validate.

     The bell atop the door jingled. In walked that white-shoed red-suited mirage known as Commie John, who seemed to be played by Uriah Bleu in box-framed glasses.

     “Greetings comrade,” Jakob and I all called.

     “Greetings vanguard,” Commie John responded. “I am happy to announce that we have received our seed monies to begin the penguin pro ‘rasslin’ league.”

     “We’re going to have penguins wrestling?” Jakob asked.

     “Well, no,” Commie John responded. “That would be inhumane. But we will have humans wrestling for the entertainment of penguins.”

     Tamaya asked: “So penguins enjoy pro ‘rasslin’?”

     “Well, not normal penguins,” Commie John. “But penguins who’ve been upgraded via installation of iContacts™ latest brain chip – they love pro ‘rasslin’. And is it so bad that they like to watch a few episodes of MondayLunarMadness™ before they go to sleep inbetween a couple of fourteen-hour workdays?”

     “Sure, that sounds reasonable,” I said. “But if they have the FansWrestlingFederation and MondayLunarMadness, why do they need whatever you’re going to do with the seed monies?”

     “Because,” Commie John said, “we’re going to bring the entertainment live to the penguins’ workplaces!”

     And then a tiny Yeshi showed up on LCDTV Cox's shoulders and Yeshi smiled.
